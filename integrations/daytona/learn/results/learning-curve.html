Same loop as reinforcement learning: the agent attempts tasks (rollouts), earns a verifiable reward from hidden tests, and its policy improves from its own trajectories. The one difference from textbook RL — the update is a durable lesson carried in context, not a weight change. Measured on a sealed held-out set, with a 95% CI on every point.

Why this isn't a hockey-stick. The gains are front-loaded — the first lesson fixes the commonest failure, each next one is rarer and worth less (diminishing returns). Two lessons were rejected: their held-out gain fell under the 4pp noise band, so they weren't credited — you can't keep an improvement you can't distinguish from noise. That's why the curve plateaus below 100%, not because the agent is perfect. To push the plateau higher you lower the noise floor — more held-out tasks and more rollouts, i.e. more Daytona. This run simulates the mechanism; the real experiment rolls out a live agent in Daytona sandboxes and grades its code against hidden tests.
